## The challenge

I joined Randstad Global as Product Design Lead in 2026 to do something most design orgs are circling but few have actually committed to: **rebuild the team's day-to-day around AI as a design partner, not just as an output engine**.

The Digital Marketplace platform is large, multi-stakeholder, and shipping continuously. The design team had the talent and the craft. What it didn't have was leverage — designers were spending most of their time on artefact production (specs, variants, handoff prep), and not enough on the parts of the job that actually move outcomes: research, framing, and strategic conversations with PMs and engineering.

The brief wasn't *"add AI to the design process"*. It was: **how do we redesign the design process itself so that AI takes the rote work and gives us back the strategic time it cost us to make that work**?

The friction points I had to attack:

- Design hours spent on production, not framing or research
- Inconsistent quality across designers using ad-hoc AI tools
- No shared vocabulary or guardrails for AI-augmented work
- PM/Design collaboration still anchored on Figma artefacts, not problems
- Hard to demonstrate the *value shift* AI was producing, beyond "it feels faster"

## Approach

### Diagnose
First six weeks: stakeholder interviews across PM, Engineering, Research, and the five designers on the team. Mapped where time was actually going (not where people *thought* it was going). The pattern was clear — designers were under-utilised on strategy precisely because they were over-utilised on production.

### Frame
Built a Design Leverage model with the team — four lanes: **Frame · Explore · Refine · Ship**. AI is heavily integrated into Explore and Refine, lighter in Frame, and barely involved in Ship (where craft and judgement matter most). The model became the shared scaffolding for talking about *where* AI belongs in any given piece of work.

### Pilot
Started with two pilots running in parallel: one inside discovery (AI-assisted research synthesis), one inside refinement (component-level AI generation feeding the design system). Tight measurement on both — time-to-insight and quality-of-insight, not just throughput.

### Roll out
Once the pilots showed signal, codified into team rituals: AI-augmented critique sessions, weekly tool reviews, a shared Prompt Vault for the team. Continuous, not one-and-done.

> Lesson from ICEO that I brought into this work: **scale the system before you scale the headcount**. Don't bolt AI onto a process that's already broken — fix the process first, then introduce AI where it adds leverage, not noise.

## What we built

**A leverage-first design process.** Frame · Explore · Refine · Ship became the team's shared model. Every project has explicit answers to *"what's the AI lane on this one?"* before work starts.

**A team Prompt Vault.** Curated, versioned, and team-owned. Prompts for research synthesis, IA generation, copy variants, and component scaffolding — each tested, rated, and tagged by the designer who built them. Stops the "everyone reinvents the same prompt" tax.

**AI-augmented rituals.** Weekly critique now includes an "AI co-critic" pass — designers prompt a model to argue against their solution before bringing it to humans. Has cut the weakest critiques out before they hit the room and made the human conversation sharper.

**A measurement frame for AI value.** Three signals tracked per project: time-to-first-prototype, designer hours on craft vs. production, and stakeholder confidence at each gate. Designed *not* to over-claim AI's contribution — half the lift is process, not tools.

**Cross-functional embedding.** Designers now sit in PM problem-framing sessions — not as receivers, but as co-framers. AI handles the artefact catch-up afterwards.

## Outcome

The early picture is encouraging. Discovery cycles roughly twice as fast. Designers reporting more time on the craft and judgement work that drew them to the field. The team is shipping more work *and* more deliberate work — not the trade-off most AI rollouts produce.

## Reflection

Two things have been true through this work:

1. **The hardest part isn't the tool, it's the process.** Most "AI for design" rollouts fail because they introduce powerful tools into a process that wasn't designed to absorb them. The team rituals, the leverage model, the shared vocabulary — all of that has done more work than any single AI tool.

2. **Designers don't want to stop doing design.** They want to stop doing the *production* parts of design that aren't design. AI hits that brief better than any productivity tool I've seen — but only when the org has decided what the designers should be doing with the time it gives back.

What's next, if I keep going on this trajectory:

- Push AI into Frame and Ship lanes — carefully — once the team is fluent in Explore/Refine
- A design-system-aware AI agent that can scaffold flows directly from the live token and component graph
- Cross-org design ops connection: link this team's Prompt Vault into a wider Randstad practice
- Continue the slow, deliberate work of demonstrating to leadership that AI-augmented design is a *quality* multiplier, not a headcount-reduction story
