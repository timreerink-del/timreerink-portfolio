## The challenge

ICEO is a venture builder operating in the fintech ecosystem, with **five ventures under one umbrella**. The company had grown explosively — from 38 to 180 employees in 18 months — but design hadn't grown with it. It had fragmented.

When I joined as Head of Design, the team was three designers working in silos under different CEOs and product managers. No shared process. No rituals. No career framework. Design was treated as an executional function — a render farm for decisions made elsewhere.

The brief was simple to state and harder to do: turn design from a fragmented, execution-driven function into a scalable, collaborative, strategic discipline that contributes to business outcomes across all five ventures.

The specific friction points I had to attack:

- No structured design process or rituals → slow delivery
- No career framework or role expectations → constant alignment overhead
- Designers micromanaged as execution resources → no time for discovery
- Limited understanding of design's strategic role → designers absent from epic creation
- Minimal collaboration between designers and stakeholders → few design thinking sessions

## Approach

### Understand
I combined desk research on design team scaling with stakeholder interviews to map pains and needs first-hand. I leaned on the Design Leadership Framework and *Design Leadership Ignited* as reference, and ran a deep analysis of the team and ICEO's broader org structure.

### Prioritise
Insights went back to stakeholders. Together we ran an impact/effort exercise — business value on one axis, feasibility on the other — to decide what to tackle first. Avoiding the "boil the ocean" trap was critical; everyone wanted everything fixed yesterday.

### Plan
With a maturity assessment in hand, I built a structured roadmap. Scaling design isn't linear — so we focused on **structure and team composition first**, before opening any new headcount. Hiring without foundations would have multiplied the chaos, not solved it.

### Execute
The shift happened in collaborative workshops with HR, Heads, Founders, Product, and Growth. Frameworks and processes weren't handed down from design — they were built *with* the people who'd have to live with them.

> One note on scope: each of the topics below deserves its own case study. I've condensed aggressively here to keep the narrative tight.

## What we built

**Team rituals and culture.** Alignment meetings, critique sessions, and a Confluence knowledge hub — all aimed at making the work visible and the learnings shareable.

**Hiring and growth.** Targeted T-shaped, startup-ready designers with stakeholder skills. Built a standardised career framework so role expectations were clear before anyone was hired or promoted.

**Scalable process.** A visualised design process clarifying roles, workflow, and time allocation — with design explicitly embedded in product strategy and discovery, not bolted on after.

**Collaboration.** Standardised feedback loops. Design thinking workshops to pull stakeholders into the process. Designers in planning sessions instead of receiving tickets.

**Leadership alignment.** Used data and process visuals to demonstrate design's strategic impact. Defined structured feedback channels with the CEO layer to cut ad-hoc interruptions.

## Outcome

Numbers moved fast once the foundation was in place. Critiques up 400%. Delivery time on design epics down 30%. Team satisfaction climbed from 4 to 6 on the Likert scale. Headcount tripled — but only after the structure could absorb it.

## Reflection

Scaling a design department is messier than any framework suggests. You're constantly trading off business pressure, time, and capacity. The biggest lesson: **scaling isn't a hiring problem, it's a structure problem.** Frameworks and rituals first, headcount second.

Leadership alignment is never finished. Even with clear processes, you have to keep educating and re-selling the strategic role of design at the executive level. Continuous, not one-and-done.

What's next, if I were still doing this work:

- A design strategy at the *macro* level of ICEO as a company
- Expanded UX research to strengthen product-market fit on the mature ventures
- Deeper DesignOps to optimise workflows at scale
- Integrating AI into the prototyping phase to speed up testing
- Continuing to refine executive alignment so design stays strategic, not decorative
